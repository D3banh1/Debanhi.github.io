# Dissertation

<details>
<summary><strong>Overview</strong></summary>

<br>
How can gender participation in security and governance reduce gender insecurity? This question guides my dissertation, constructed as a series of three papers. Across Latin America, there have been substantial institutional innovations to address the chronic violence women face. While the "feminization" of male-dominated spaces has been built around the notion that female representation in security forces and in political leadership would lead to significant changes to women's lives, gender insecurity and gender-based violence rates are still increasing. My first project asks: Under what conditions does state repression fluctuate given the gender composition of police forces? My second asks: How do non-state forms of governance produce different conditions of insecurity for women? The third is focused on empirically testing how women's empowerment in governance helps to curb gender-based violence

### The Impact of Gender Empowerment on Gender Violence 

Debanhi Rojas and Alison Brysk (2026)

### Can Indigenous Political Autonomy Impact Gender-Based Violence? Evidence from Mexico

<details>
<summary><strong>Abstract</strong></summary>

<br>

How do non-state forms of governance produce different conditions of insecurity for women? Although
evidence suggests that traditional governance can outperform formal institutions in resisting crime and
delivering public goods (Magaloni et al., 2019; Magaloni et al., 2021), little is known about how variation
within traditional governance shapes these outcomes. Consequently, this project examines the hetero-
geneity of indigenous communities with traditional governance by testing whether the strength of their
gender norms experience higher rates of femicide in Oaxaca, Mexico. Using data from the Catálogo Na-
cional de Pueblos y Comunidades Indígenas y Afromexicanas (CANAPIA 2026), this project argues that
formal legal recognition of self-governance reduces femicide rates compared to party-governed localities,
but that this protective effect is not uniform across all traditional governance communities. I test this
argument through two steps. First, I use a geographic regression discontinuity design along a boundary
between localities that hold formal legal recognition of the right to self-governance and those that do
not, a distinction conferred by Oaxaca’s 1995 constitutional reform, to estimate the average effect of
indigenous governance on femicide rates. Second, I examine heterogeneous treatment effects by testing
whether this effect is conditioned by the degree to which women are incorporated into community po-
litical structures. I argue that these systems are not uniformly effective in providing security to women
and that institutions capable of deterring crime can simultaneously reproduce the gendered exclusions
that enable femicide.
